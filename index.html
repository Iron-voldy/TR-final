Question 1 – Reading Comprehension: Quantum Computing (25 marks)

Recommended time: 25 minutes
Passage: The Quantum Computing Revolution

Quantum computing represents a paradigm shift in computational technology, leveraging quantum mechanical phenomena to process information in fundamentally different ways than classical computers. Unlike traditional bits that exist in definite states of 0 or 1, quantum bits (qubits) can exist in superposition, allowing them to represent multiple states simultaneously.

The potential applications of quantum computing are vast and transformative. In cryptography, quantum computers could break current encryption methods while simultaneously enabling quantum encryption protocols that are theoretically unbreakable. Drug discovery could be revolutionized through quantum simulations of molecular interactions, potentially reducing development timeframes from decades to years.

However, significant technical challenges persist. Quantum states are extremely fragile and susceptible to environmental interference, a phenomenon known as decoherence. Current quantum computers require near-absolute-zero temperatures and sophisticated error correction systems. Despite these obstacles, major technology companies and governments are investing billions in quantum research, recognizing its potential to solve problems currently beyond classical computing capabilities.

Question 2 – Summary Writing: Edge Computing (15 marks)

Recommended time: 20 minutes
Edge Computing: Bringing Processing Closer to Data Sources

Edge computing represents a distributed computing paradigm that brings computation and data storage closer to data sources and end-users. This approach reduces latency by processing data locally rather than sending it to centralized cloud servers. Industries like autonomous vehicles, healthcare monitoring, and industrial IoT benefit significantly from real-time processing capabilities.

The architecture of edge computing involves deploying small data centers and processing units at network edges. These edge nodes can make immediate decisions without relying on distant cloud infrastructure. This decentralization improves response times, reduces bandwidth usage, and enhances privacy by keeping sensitive data local.

However, edge computing presents unique challenges including device management complexity, security concerns across distributed nodes, and standardization issues. Organizations must balance the benefits of reduced latency with increased infrastructure management overhead and potential security vulnerabilities.

Question 1 – Reading Comprehension: Climate Technology (25 marks)

Recommended time: 25 minutes
Passage: Carbon Capture and Storage Technology

Carbon capture and storage (CCS) technology represents one of the most promising approaches to mitigating climate change by preventing CO₂ emissions from reaching the atmosphere. This technology captures carbon dioxide from industrial processes and power plants, then transports and stores it permanently underground in geological formations.

Three main types of CCS exist: post-combustion capture, pre-combustion capture, and oxy-fuel combustion. Post-combustion capture removes CO₂ from flue gases after fuel burning, while pre-combustion capture converts fuel into hydrogen and CO₂ before combustion. Oxy-fuel combustion burns fuel in pure oxygen, producing a concentrated CO₂ stream for easier capture.

Despite its potential, CCS faces significant economic and technical barriers. Current costs range from $50-100 per ton of CO₂ captured, making widespread deployment economically challenging. Additionally, long-term storage safety concerns and limited suitable geological sites pose implementation obstacles. However, government incentives and technological advances are gradually reducing costs and improving efficiency.

Question 1 – Reading Comprehension: AI Ethics and Bias (25 marks)

Recommended time: 25 minutes
Passage: Addressing Algorithmic Bias in AI Systems

Algorithmic bias in artificial intelligence systems has become a critical concern as AI increasingly influences decisions in hiring, lending, criminal justice, and healthcare. These biases often reflect historical inequalities present in training data, perpetuating discrimination against marginalized groups even when algorithms appear objective.

Several types of bias affect AI systems. Historical bias occurs when training data reflects past discriminatory practices, while representation bias emerges when certain groups are underrepresented in datasets. Measurement bias arises from differences in data quality across populations, and evaluation bias results from using inappropriate benchmarks for different demographic groups.

Addressing algorithmic bias requires multifaceted approaches including diverse development teams, bias testing throughout the development lifecycle, and ongoing monitoring of deployed systems. Regulatory frameworks like the EU's AI Act are establishing accountability standards, while technical solutions include fairness-aware algorithms and bias mitigation techniques. However, completely eliminating bias remains challenging, as value judgments about fairness vary across cultures and contexts.

🎓 Expert Exam Strategies

Advanced Techniques for Top Performance:

📊 Data Analysis Questions: Always start with overview, identify the highest and lowest values, look for trends over time, and conclude with implications.

🔄 Process Descriptions: Use varied time connectors (Initially, Subsequently, Following this, Simultaneously, Ultimately), include purpose statements, and maintain consistent technical vocabulary.

📝 Business Correspondence: Match tone to audience, use active voice, include specific details and deadlines, and always proofread for professional presentation.

📖 Reading Comprehension: Preview questions before reading, underline key terms, look for signal words (however, therefore, nevertheless), and eliminate obviously wrong answers first.

⏰ Time Management Pro Tips: Spend 2-3 minutes planning longer responses, leave difficult questions for the end, and always reserve 5 minutes for final review.
